Barbara Jordan

The first Black woman ever elected to Congress from the south. She was a teacher, lawyer, and politician that was known for giving impactful speeches which advocated for civil rights and social justice. Her most impactful speech was when she addressed the Judiciary Committee on the impeachment of former President Richard Nixon.

  • Office

    Representative

  • State/Territory

    Texas

  • Party

    Democrat

  • Congress(es)

    93rd (1973-1975), 94th (1975-1977), 95th (1977-1979)

Time Line

February 21, 1936

Born in Houston Texas. Barbara Jordan grew up during the era of the Jim crow system. However, this unjust system did not stop Jordan from getting an education and becoming a lawyer.

1956

Graduated from Texas Southern University. She studied history and political science, preparing herself for law school.

1959

Graduated from Boston University Law School

1960

Worked on the Kennedy-Johnson campaign

1962, 1964

Ran unsuccessfully for election to the Texas House of Representatives

1966

Elected to the Texas Senate becoming the first African American state senator since 1883

1967

-Became first Black elected official to preside over the Texas Senate. This means she had a highly influential role in the legislative process. She was the first Black state senator to chair a major committee, Labor and Management. This meant she had a great influence on in legislative matters related to labor and management

1972

Elected to the United States House of Representatives for Houston's 18th district, becoming the first black woman from a Southern state to serve in the House.

1974

Made the historic speech before the House of Judiciary Committee supporting the impeachment of President Nixon

1976

Made the historic keynote address at the Democratic National Convention

1976

Sponsored legislation broaden the Voting Rights Act of 1965 to cover Mexican Americans in Texas and other southwestern states. This extended its authority to those states where minorities had been denied the right to vote or had their rights restricted by unfair registration practices, such as literacy test

1977

Sponsored the Community Reinvestment Act of 1977, legislation that required banks to lend and make services available to undeserved poor and minority communities.

1979

Retired from politics in 1979 and became a professor at the University of Texas at Austin Lyndon B. Johnson School of Public Affairs

1992

Keynote speaker at the Democratic National Convention

1995

Awarded the prestigious United States Military Academy's Sylvanus Thayer Award

January 17, 1996

Died
